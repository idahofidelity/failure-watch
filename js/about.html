WHAT THIS IS

Failure Watch is a public-interest tracker documenting US industrial incidents — explosions, fires, spills, leaks, infrastructure failures, and attacks — at chemical plants, oil refineries, pipelines, rail lines, bridges, and power facilities from 2010 to present.

The purpose is transparency. Mainstream media covers only the most dramatic incidents. Federal agencies track some but not all. Activist organizations track incidents selectively to advance specific narratives. Failure Watch cross-references all of these and fills the gaps — presenting incidents factually, without political framing, with sourced articles rated for neutrality.

This is an Idaho Fidelity Foundation project. IFF is a conservative, Idaho-first public interest organization committed to government accountability, infrastructure transparency, and data-driven reporting.

DATA SOURCES

Failure Watch pulls from federal agencies, news sources, and activist trackers. Each source is labeled so users know the origin and potential framing of data.

CSB — Chemical Safety Board Federal
Independent federal agency that investigates chemical accidents. Most authoritative source for cause determinations. Investigations are thorough, slow, and politically neutral.
csb.gov
PHMSA — Pipeline Safety Federal
DOT agency tracking pipeline incidents, corrective action orders, and inspection records. Most comprehensive pipeline safety database in the US.
phmsa.dot.gov
EPA RMP — Risk Management Federal
EPA's Risk Management Plan database. Facilities storing hazardous chemicals above threshold quantities must file. Includes accident history and emergency response plans.
epa.gov/rmp
NTSB — Transportation Safety Federal
Investigates rail and pipeline transportation accidents. Authoritative for rail tanker derailments and pipeline incidents involving transportation.
ntsb.gov
NRC — Nuclear Regulatory Commission Federal
Event notifications and inspection findings for all US nuclear facilities. Tracks unusual events, radiation releases, and license violations.
nrc.gov
OSHA — Workplace Safety Federal
Inspection records, citations, and severe violator program data for chemical and industrial facilities. Public via OSHA data portal.
osha.gov
EIA — Energy Information Administration Federal
Refinery age, capacity, and operational data. Used to identify aging facilities approaching industry failure thresholds.
eia.gov
Google News RSS News
Wide-net news scraper pulling from thousands of local and national sources using industrial incident trigger keywords. Catches incidents missed by federal databases.
news.google.com
SpillTracker.org Cross-Ref Only
Bloomberg-funded environmental activist tracker. Used for cross-reference only. Known for left-leaning framing and selective incident coverage. Listed on pins where applicable so users can compare coverage.
spilltracker.org
Coalition to Prevent Chemical Disasters Cross-Ref Only
Environmental NGO tracker. Used for cross-reference only. Environmental advocacy framing. Listed on pins where applicable.
chemicaldisasters.org
NEWS ARTICLE SOURCING & BIAS RATING

Each incident pin links to up to 5 news articles. Articles are pulled from GNews (which indexes 80,000+ sources including local papers) and rated for neutrality by AI before display.

Rating scale:

RatingWhat It MeansTypical Sources
✓ NeutralFactual reporting, minimal framingAP, Reuters, NPR, local TV affiliates, government agencies
~ SlightMinor framing present but informativeRegional newspapers, trade publications
⚠ BiasedSignificant advocacy framingActivist org sites, heavily partisan outlets

Neutral-rated articles are shown first. Fallback articles (pre-researched by IFF staff) are used when live search returns no results.

AT-RISK FACILITIES LAYER

The at-risk layer shows facilities that have NOT necessarily had an incident but are flagged based on federal criteria:

Age thresholds — Industry standard design life is 30-50 years for most process equipment. The American Petroleum Institute (API), ASME, and PHMSA all publish standards on inspection intervals and expected service life. Facilities approaching or exceeding these thresholds are flagged.

Inspection failures — OSHA citations, PHMSA corrective action orders, EPA enforcement actions, and NRC violation notices are all public record. Facilities with active orders are flagged.

Planned repairs — SEC 10-K and 10-Q filings by public companies disclose mandatory repair and upgrade programs. These are included where available.

⚠ Presence on the at-risk layer does not mean a facility is unsafe or will fail. It means it meets criteria that federal standards identify as warranting heightened scrutiny. All facilities on this layer are operating legally.

HOW DATA IS UPDATED
WEEKLY
Automated scraper runs every Sunday night. Pulls from CSB, PHMSA, NRC, EPA ECHO, OSHA, and Google News RSS across 14 trigger keyword queries. New incidents are automatically committed to GitHub and appear on the map within minutes.
MANUAL
IFF staff manually review and add major incidents that require more detailed research — cause determination, financial figures, and cross-referencing against federal agency databases.
FINANCIAL
Damage and cleanup cost figures are added only when confirmed by federal agency reports, court records, or company filings. Estimated figures are clearly labeled. All amounts shown in both as-reported and 2025 inflation-adjusted dollars.
COVERAGE GAP DISCLOSURE

No single database captures all US industrial incidents. Failure Watch is more comprehensive than any single federal or activist source but is not exhaustive. Known limitations:

• Small incidents with no injuries, no evacuation, and no media coverage often go unreported in all databases. PHMSA and OSHA require reporting above certain thresholds — below-threshold incidents may not be captured.

• State-level incidents (regulated by state agencies rather than federal) vary significantly in reporting quality by state.

• The at-risk facility layer is limited to facilities with public federal data. Private companies with no EPA RMP filing, no SEC disclosure, and no PHMSA records may be absent.

• Historical data (2010-2018) relies more heavily on news sources and Wikipedia than the more recent data, which has fuller federal agency cross-referencing.
